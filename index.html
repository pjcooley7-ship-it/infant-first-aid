01Infant CPR (0–12 months)

Used when a baby is unresponsive and not breathing normally (or only gasping). Less common than choking, but highest-cost if you don't know it. Follows 2025 AHA/AAP guidelines.

Key principle
Adult cardiac arrest is a heart problem. Infant cardiac arrest is usually a breathing problem — that's why infant CPR keeps rescue breaths (not compression-only like adults).

When to start CPR

  • Unresponsive — no reaction when you tap the foot firmly and shout
  • Not breathing, or only gasping (irregular gasps ≠ normal breathing)
  • You've checked for no more than 10 seconds

If breathing but unresponsive: recovery position (on side), keep airway open, call 144, stay with them. Do NOT start CPR on a breathing baby.

The sequence

Step 1 · Check & call
  • Tap sole of foot firmly + shout their name
  • If someone else is there: have them call 144 immediately
  • If alone: do 2 minutes of CPR FIRST, then call 144 on speakerphone. In infants, oxygen is the limiting factor.
Step 2 · Open airway
  • Firm flat surface — floor beats bed/sofa
  • Gentle head tilt — neutral or slightly extended. Don't overextend. Babies have large heads; tilting too far closes the airway
  • Look in mouth — if you SEE an object, pinky-sweep. Never blind-sweep.
Step 3 · 2 rescue breaths
  • Seal your mouth over baby's mouth AND nose (they're small — yours covers both)
  • Small cheek puffs, not lung breaths. Just enough for chest to gently rise. Full adult breaths will injure infant lungs.
  • Each breath ≈ 1 second. Watch chest rise, let fall before next
  • If chest doesn't rise: reposition head and try once. Still no rise → treat as airway obstruction, skip to compressions
Step 4 · 30 chest compressions
⚠ Technique changed in 2025
The two-finger method is no longer recommended. For a lone rescuer: heel of one hand, lower half of sternum, just below nipple line.
LocationLower half of sternum, just below imaginary nipple line
Depth~4 cm (1.5 in) — one-third chest depth
Rate100–120/min · tempo of "Baby Shark" or "Stayin' Alive"
RecoilFull recoil between compressions — don't lean
Step 5 · Cycles of 30:2
  • Ratio is 30 compressions + 2 breaths, repeat
  • Don't stop to check breathing / pulse between cycles
  • Keep going until: baby breathes, help takes over, or you can't continue
  • Alone? After 2 min (~5 cycles): pause, call 144 on speakerphone, resume while on call

AED (defibrillator)

  • Modern AEDs work on infants. Use paediatric pads if included.
  • Only adult pads available? Use them — one chest, one back. Any shock > no shock.
  • Follow the voice prompts. Resume CPR immediately after any shock.
Common mistakes that kill effectiveness
  • Compressing too shallow (fear of hurting baby — cracked ribs are acceptable; no blood flow isn't)
  • Overextending the head (closes the airway in babies)
  • Blowing too hard (cheek puffs, not lung breaths)
  • Stopping too often to check
02Choking

The emergency you're most likely to face — especially once your baby starts solids around 6 months. Small pieces + a developing swallow = real risk. Most choking is resolved quickly if you act correctly.

The critical distinction: gagging vs. choking

GAGGING (LOUD)
  • Coughing, spluttering, retching
  • NOISY — you can hear it
  • Red/watery face
  • Tongue pushing forward
  • Baby is actively working
✓ Do not intervene. Stay close and watch.
CHOKING (SILENT)
  • Silent — no cry, cough, sound
  • Can't breathe or make noise
  • Pale / grey / blue lips
  • Panic in eyes
  • Weak ineffective coughing
✗ Back blows + chest thrusts NOW.
Gagging is a protective reflex
Baby's gag reflex sits further forward on the tongue than yours. You'll see lots of gagging in the first weeks of solids. Don't clap their back, don't pick them up, don't finger-sweep — just watch calmly.

Choking sequence (under 1 year)

Do NOT do abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) on an infant
2025 AHA guidelines explicitly exclude Heimlich for under-1s — it injures organs in small bodies. Use back blows + chest thrusts only.
Step 1 · Shout for help
  • Someone else there? Have them call 144 immediately.
  • Alone? Start maneuvers first, call if obstruction doesn't clear.
Step 2 · 5 back blows
  • Sit. Baby face-DOWN along your forearm, head LOWER than body
  • Support chest + jaw with hand (fingers clear of throat)
  • Rest forearm on thigh
  • Heel of free hand: 5 firm blows between shoulder blades — firm, not gentle
  • Check after each — did object come out?
Step 3 · 5 chest thrusts
  • Flip baby face-UP along other forearm. Head still lower than body.
  • Heel of one hand, lower sternum (same landmark as CPR)
  • 5 sharp thrusts, ~4 cm deep — faster & sharper than CPR compressions
  • Check mouth. See object? Pinky-sweep. Never blind-sweep.
Step 4 · Repeat

Alternate 5 back blows / 5 chest thrusts until:

  • Object out → baby coughs/cries/breathes
  • Baby becomes unresponsive → stop choking protocol, start CPR
  • Help arrives

Alone and not clearing after 1 min? Pause, call 144 on speakerphone, resume while on call. Always see a doctor after a significant choking event — residual swelling or fragments.

Choking hazards (up-to-date)

AVOID ENTIRELY (<12 mo)
  • Whole nuts, popcorn
  • Whole grapes, cherries, cherry tomatoes
  • Hard sweets, marshmallows
  • Raw carrot/apple sticks
  • Chunks of hard cheese
  • Globs of nut butter off a spoon
  • Round slices of Wienerli/sausage
MODIFY BEFORE SERVING
  • Grapes & tomatoes → QUARTER lengthwise
  • Sausages → strips, not coins
  • Carrot/apple → steam soft or shred
  • Nuts → ground/butter thinned
  • Blueberries → squish until ~10 mo
  • Bread → toast first (doughy = sticks)
Top safety rules
  • Round is dangerous. Round + smooth + sized like a baby airway = coins of grape, whole blueberries. Always quarter lengthwise, never slice into coins.
  • Always supervise. Upright high chair. No eating in stroller/car seat/lying down.
  • Don't prop a bottle. Never leave baby alone with one; aspiration risk.
  • Rule of thumb: smaller than a 5-franc coin + cylindrical/round = modify first.
03Allergic reactions & anaphylaxis

You'll introduce all 8 major allergens (egg, peanut, dairy, wheat, sesame, tree nut, fish, soy) in the first 4–6 weeks of starting solids. Most reactions (if any) are mild — but you need to recognize anaphylaxis fast because epinephrine is time-sensitive.

Mild reaction (call Kinderärztin, business hours)

  • Isolated hives in one area
  • Single episode of vomiting (no other symptoms)
  • Mild swelling of one lip / one eyelid
  • Eczema flare on face after new food
  • Redness only around mouth is usually NOT allergy — acidic foods + friction from smearing. ASCIA says don't let this alone stop re-offering the food.
🚨 ANAPHYLAXIS — call 144

Key rule: mild symptoms in > one body system (skin + gut, skin + breathing) = anaphylaxis. Don't wait for it to worsen.

In infants, anaphylaxis is usually hives (94%) + vomiting (83%). Breathing symptoms less common than in adults (~17%) — don't wait for wheezing.

Other danger signs: sudden lethargy/limpness, wheezing or stridor, tongue/throat swelling, pale or blue-grey skin, collapse.

Most reactions begin 5–30 min after exposure.

What to do

  1. Call 144. Say "Suspected anaphylaxis in an infant."
  2. EpiPen Junior in outer thigh (through clothing, 3 sec) if prescribed. Note the time.
  3. Lay baby on side if vomiting; otherwise flat, legs slightly elevated.
  4. Stay with them. Be ready to start CPR if unresponsive.
  5. No antihistamines as substitute for epinephrine in a severe reaction — too slow. Antihistamines are for mild reactions only.
  6. Don't induce vomiting — worsens airway compromise.

How to introduce allergens safely

  • One new allergen at a time, morning or midday — NOT evening. Be awake and near help.
  • Small amount first (lick, half-tsp). Tolerated after 15 min? Give full serving.
  • Stay within 30 min of home/car during first exposure.
  • Observe ~2 hours. Most reactions happen in first 30 min, but delays occur.
  • Maintain 2–3×/week after introduction. Ongoing exposure maintains tolerance. Introducing once then stopping can worsen risk.
04Burns, scalds, and falls

Burns & scalds

Scalds from hot liquids are the most common household burn in infants — coffee, tea, bathwater. Babies' skin is thinner than adults' → same liquid causes deeper burns faster.

Any burn — what to do
  1. Cool burn under cool (not cold) running water for 20 min. Single most important step. Effective up to 3 hrs after. NOT ice — damages tissue.
  2. Remove clothing/jewellery from area — UNLESS stuck to skin. Don't peel stuck fabric.
  3. Cover loosely with cling film (Frischhaltefolie) or clean non-fluffy cloth after cooling. Cling film is ideal — sterile-ish, non-stick, medics can see through.
  4. No creams, butter, toothpaste, aloe. Just water → cling film.
  5. Keep baby warm (wrap rest of body) while cooling the burn — hypothermia is a real risk.
Call 144 or go to ER for
  • Burn on face, hands, feet, genitals, over a joint
  • Larger than baby's palm (~1% BSA)
  • Blistered, white, or charred skin (2nd/3rd degree)
  • Chemical or electrical burn
  • Any burn on a <12mo — err on calling at least Kinderärztin/Permanence
Prevention — highest-value changes
  • Never hold baby while drinking hot drinks. Wriggling + grabbing a mug = some of the worst scald cases.
  • Bathwater: 37°C max. Bath thermometer or elbow test (your hand is less sensitive than baby's skin). Cold first, then add hot.
  • Pot handles turned inward. Don't carry hot through rooms with baby on floor.
  • Microwave-heated bottles heat unevenly — hotspots burn baby's mouth even if bottle feels lukewarm. Use bottle warmer or jug of warm water.

Falls & head bumps

Risk grows as baby develops — rolling (4mo+), sitting (6mo+), crawling (7–9mo). Changing-table fall and sofa roll are most common.

Call 144 immediately if
  • Loss of consciousness, even briefly
  • More than one vomit
  • Seizure / convulsion
  • Unequal pupils, or pupils don't respond to light
  • Clear fluid or blood from nose/ear
  • Unusual sleepiness / can't rouse baby
  • Bulging soft spot (fontanelle)
  • Fall from >2× baby's height (~120 cm) — regardless of symptoms
Call Kinderärztin (non-urgent) if
  • Visible bump/bruise but alert and normal behaviour
  • Brief crying that settles, then back to themselves
  • Any concern you can't articulate — parental instinct is valid data
At home after minor bump
  • Cold pack wrapped in cloth, 10–15 min. Never ice directly on skin.
  • Observe 24h. Let baby sleep — old "keep awake" advice is outdated — but wake every 3–4 hrs first night to confirm rousable and responsive.
  • Watch for red flags appearing in the hours after.
05Seizures, fever, and illness

Febrile seizures

Occur in 2–5% of kids 6mo–5yr, triggered by rapidly rising fever. Terrifying to watch but almost always benign — single febrile seizure doesn't cause brain damage. Usually 1–3 min.

What it looks like
  • Sudden stiffening then rhythmic jerking of arms/legs
  • Eyes rolling back or staring
  • Loss of consciousness
  • May lose bladder control
  • Skin pale or slightly blue
  • Afterwards: drowsy/confused 10–30 min
What to do
  1. Stay calm. Note the time. Duration is the single most important piece of info for the doctor.
  2. Lay baby on side on soft surface, clear of hard/sharp objects. Loosen tight clothing.
  3. DO NOT: put anything in their mouth (they won't bite tongue — myth); restrain them; give medication during seizure; cold-bath them.
  4. Watch and wait. Most stop on their own in 1–3 min.
  5. After: keep on side until fully alert. Then call Kinderärztin or go to ER — a first febrile seizure always warrants assessment.
🚨 Call 144 during a seizure if
  • Lasts >5 minutes
  • Difficulty breathing, lips turning blue
  • Second seizure right after the first
  • Injured during the seizure
  • Doesn't wake up after it ends
  • First-ever seizure — safest to call 144

Fever

Fever = rectal ≥38.0°C (100.4°F) or armpit ≥37.5°C. Fever itself isn't dangerous — but in very young infants can be the only sign of serious infection.

Call 144 / Kinderärztin IMMEDIATELY for
  • Any fever ≥38.0°C in <3 months — no exceptions
  • Fever ≥39.0°C in 3–6 months
  • Fever + extreme drowsiness/limpness
  • Fever + stiff neck, non-blanching rash (doesn't fade when pressed with glass — meningitis warning)
  • Dehydration: no wet diaper 6+ hrs, sunken fontanelle, no tears
  • Difficulty breathing, grunting, fast breathing
  • Fever >3 days
At home (older infants, not seriously unwell)
  • Paracetamol (Dafalgan Baby suppositories) — safe from birth. Dose by weight.
  • Ibuprofen (Algifor) — from 3 months AND >5 kg. Dose by weight. Never combine with paracetamol unless told to.
  • Never give aspirin — Reye's syndrome risk.
  • Hydration: extra feeds, small frequent offers
  • Dress lightly — no bundling. Room ~18–20°C.
Other illness red flags — trust your instincts
  • Unusual cry (high-pitched, weak, inconsolable for hours)
  • Extremely floppy / unresponsive
  • Fast, hard breathing with visible rib retraction
  • Persistent vomiting (especially projectile)
  • Blood in vomit, stool, or urine
  • Refuses feeds >6 hrs
  • Significantly fewer wet diapers
  • Non-blanching rash anywhere
  • Your instinct says something's wrong — even without clear symptoms
When to call what
  • 144: Life-threatening or unsafe to transport yourself
  • Kinderärztin: Worrying but stable. Most practices have a day/out-of-hours line.
  • Permanence: Walk-in urgent care. Kinderärztlicher Notfalldienst Zürich at Kinderspital covers evenings/weekends.
  • medi24 (0800 33 66 55): 24/7 nurse advice line, free from Swiss landlines.
  • CSS Kids Line (0800 44 43 33): 24/7 paediatric phone advice — but only if you have CSS basic insurance. Often resolves the question entirely without needing an in-person visit.
06Poisoning & Tox Info Suisse

Tox Info Suisse (145) is the Swiss national poison info centre — staffed 24/7 by physicians. 40% of their calls involve pre-school children, almost all accidental. Heavily used by parents, and free.

145 vs. 144 — how to decide
  • Call 144 FIRST if baby is unconscious, can't breathe, seizing, or severely unwell. Don't wait on hold with 145 if baby is deteriorating.
  • Call 145 if you suspect ingestion but baby is alert, breathing normally, otherwise well. Physicians assess and tell you whether to monitor at home, go to ER, or escalate to 144.
  • Most of the time, 145 can reassure you. Keep both numbers saved.

What to do if poisoning suspected

  1. Seriously unwell → call 144.
  2. Stable → call 145. Have ready: what was taken (product or plant name — read the package), how much (estimate), when, baby's weight & age, symptoms so far.
  3. Take the product with you if going to ER, or photograph the label (front, back, ingredients).
  4. Don't induce vomiting. Syrup of ipecac is no longer recommended and is dangerous.
  5. Don't give milk, water, or anything by mouth unless Tox Info or 144 specifically says so. Some substances react with liquids.
  6. Don't wait for symptoms. Many poisonings have a delay.

Top-risk items (worth securing NOW)

  • Button/coin batteries (Knopfbatterien) — can burn through the esophagus in 2 hours. Found in key fobs, remotes, toys, scales, LED candles. Suspected ingestion = call 144 immediately, not 145 — always time-critical ER.
  • Dishwasher tabs, laundry pods — alkaline, cause severe mouth/esophageal burns. High cupboard or locked.
  • Paracetamol + ibuprofen (including yours). Overdose → liver failure with minimal early symptoms.
  • Essential oils — especially eucalyptus, camphor, tea tree, wintergreen. Tiny amounts cause seizures in infants. Store out of reach, never apply undiluted.
  • Drain/oven cleaner — extreme alkalis, chemical burns.
  • Nicotine (vape liquid) — fatal in tiny amounts for an infant.
  • Iron supplements — look like candy. Commonly overlooked.

07International travel with an infant

Travelling internationally changes your emergency landscape. Different numbers, different healthcare systems, different languages. The principles below apply to any international trip; specific examples use the US since it's a common destination from Switzerland.

On the plane

  • Notify cabin crew immediately in any emergency — they have a medical kit, defibrillator, and can page any doctor on board. Most long-haul flights have ≥1 medical professional among passengers.
  • Airlines have standing arrangements with ground-based medical advisors (MedAire, STAT-MD) who are called for in-flight emergencies.
  • If your baby is unresponsive or choking, start the maneuver yourself — your 10 seconds matter more than waiting for cabin crew.
  • Emergency landing is a real option if a doctor on board recommends it. Don't hesitate to escalate.

Verify your destination's number before travelling — these can change. The international SOS number 112 works on most GSM mobile phones globally even without local SIM or signal to your home network.

Before any international trip — checklist

  • Save destination's emergency + poison numbers in your phone before you fly.
  • Verify roaming on your mobile plan for the destination country.
  • Carry a paper note: baby's full name, date of birth, insurance details, paediatrician contact, any medical issues. You may not be able to unlock your phone.
  • Travel insurance — verify coverage for your destination, especially the US (some Swiss supplementary insurances explicitly exclude the US due to cost). Carry the card and policy number.
  • Locate the nearest children's hospital to where you'll be staying. In major cities you'll usually find a dedicated paediatric ER.
  • Pack infant paracetamol or local equivalent (acetaminophen in US/Canada), a thermometer, and oral rehydration sachets. Don't assume your preferred brands are easy to find abroad.

Worked example — travelling to the USA

  • 911 — universal US emergency (ambulance, police, fire).
  • 1-800-222-1222 — Poison Control, 24/7, free.
  • For non-emergency care: "urgent care centers" are the US equivalent of Swiss Permanences — much faster and cheaper than ERs for non-life-threatening issues (fevers, minor injuries, rashes).
  • Children's hospitals in major US cities have dedicated paediatric ERs. Examples: Children's Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP), Boston Children's, Texas Children's, Lurie (Chicago), Seattle Children's.
US healthcare — what to expect

Unlike Switzerland, the US has no universal healthcare. ERs are legally required to stabilise anyone regardless of insurance, so do not hesitate in a real emergency. But expect: significant billing, insurance paperwork afterward, and long non-urgent waits.

For non-emergency care, urgent care centers are much faster and cheaper than ERs.

Most travel insurances (including Swiss ones with US coverage) reimburse after the fact. Keep all itemised bills and receipts.
